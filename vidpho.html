▶ Shapes of Magnetic Field

Straight Current-Carrying Wire

The magnetic field forms concentric circular lines around the wire.

• Very strong near the wire
• Becomes weaker as we move away
• Direction depends on current direction

Circular Coil

The magnetic field is strongest at the center of the coil.

• Lines are dense at the center
• Field decreases away from the center
• Similar to a short solenoid

Solenoid Coil

The magnetic field inside the solenoid is uniform.

• Strong and parallel inside
• Weak and spread outside
• Acts like a bar magnet

▶ Physics Rules Images

Ampere Right Hand Rule

Used to determine the direction of magnetic field around a straight current-carrying conductor.

• Thumb → direction of current
• Fingers → direction of magnetic field

Right Cork Screw Rule

Used to find the direction of magnetic field produced by a solenoid.

• Rotation → current direction
• Movement → magnetic field direction

Clockwise Rule

Used to determine the polarity of a coil.

• Clockwise → South pole
• Anti-clockwise → North pole

Fleming Left Hand Rule

Used to find the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor.

• First finger → magnetic field
• Second finger → current
• Thumb → force

Fleming Right Hand Rule

Used to find the direction of induced current in a generator.

• Thumb → motion
• First finger → magnetic field
• Second finger → induced current

Lenz’s Law

Determines the direction of induced current.

• Opposes the change in magnetic flux
• Explains direction of induction

▶ Physics Equations (Chapter 1 & 2)

Magnetic Field due to Straight Wire

B = μ₀ I / (2πr)

• B ∝ current strength
• B ∝ 1 / distance

Magnetic Field at Center of Circular Coil

B = μ₀ N I / (2R)

• Increases with number of turns
• Strongest at the center

Magnetic Field inside Solenoid

B = μ₀ n I

• Uniform inside solenoid
• Depends on turns per length

Magnetic Force on a Wire

F = B I L sinθ

• Maximum when θ = 90°
• Zero when wire is parallel

Magnetic Torque on a Coil

τ = N B I A sinθ

• Causes rotation of the coil
• Basis of electric motors

Faraday’s Law of Induction

ε = − dΦ / dt

• EMF depends on flux change
• Negative sign from Lenz’s law

Mutual Induction

ε = − M (dI / dt)

• One coil affects another
• Depends on mutual inductance

Self Induction

ε = − L (dI / dt)

• Coil resists current change
• L is self inductance

Transformer Equation

V₁ / V₂ = N₁ / N₂

• Works with AC only
• Based on electromagnetic induction
